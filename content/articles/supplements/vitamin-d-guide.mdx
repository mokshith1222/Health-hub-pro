## What is Vitamin D?

Vitamin D is a fat-soluble vitamin that acts more like a hormone in your body. It's produced when your skin is exposed to sunlight and is also available through certain foods and supplements.

## Key Functions of Vitamin D

### Bone Health
Vitamin D regulates calcium absorption, which is essential for:
- Bone formation and mineralization
- Reducing osteoporosis risk
- Maintaining bone density

### Immune Function
Studies show adequate vitamin D levels support:
- Enhanced immune response
- Reduced infection risk
- Improved respiratory health

### Cellular Function
- Regulates cell growth
- Reduces inflammation
- Supports muscle function

**Source:** National Institutes of Health, 2024

## Recommended Dosage

The Endocrine Society recommends:

| Age Group | Daily Requirement | Upper Limit |
|-----------|------------------|-------------|
| 0-12 months | 400-1000 IU | 2000 IU |
| 1-18 years | 600-1000 IU | 4000 IU |
| 19-70 years | 1500-2000 IU | 4000 IU |
| 70+ years | 1500-2000 IU | 4000 IU |

## Natural Sources of Vitamin D

### High Vitamin D Foods
- Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines): 600-1000 IU per serving
- Egg yolks: 40-60 IU per egg
- Fortified milk: 100-200 IU per cup
- Fortified cereals: 40-100 IU per serving
- Mushrooms (sun-exposed): 100-1600 IU per 100g

### Sun Exposure
Moderate sun exposure (10-30 minutes, 3-4 times weekly) helps your skin produce vitamin D naturally.

## When to Supplement

Consider vitamin D supplementation if you:
- Live in areas with limited sunlight
- Have dark skin (requires more sun exposure)
- Follow a vegetarian/vegan diet
- Have malabsorption issues
- Work primarily indoors
- Have diagnosed deficiency

## Conclusion

Vitamin D is crucial for overall health. Whether through sunlight, food sources, or supplements, maintaining adequate levels is important for bone health, immune function, and general wellness.
